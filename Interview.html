The Interview

Getting to Know Dylan Rhee

Q: What was your role in South Sudan, and what did the job involve day to day?
I was part of the Korean contingent of UNMISS — the United Nations Mission in South Sudan. I served as a civil affairs specialist, deployed in the town of Pibor and attached to a small team of military engineers. My primary job was to act as a liaison between the military side and the civilian branches of the UN. On a practical level, that meant organizing the escort of civilians under infantry protection and serving as the communicator for infantry soldiers during those operations.
Q: Were you ever in danger? Did you experience combat firsthand?
There were plenty of mortars and bullets landing nearby. It's the kind of thing that shocks you at first, but honestly, you get used to it. It becomes part of the background of daily life out there.
Q: What motivated you to volunteer for a peacekeeping mission in South Sudan?
It looked fun — it seemed like a real adventure. I thought to myself, when else would I ever visit South Sudan? The training alone was fascinating. I received interpreter training and UN peacekeeping coursework covering international law, how to interact with local populations, and even learned a couple of phrases in Dinka, the local language. I had to carry a handbook with key phrases like "Stop moving or we will shoot" and "Leave the area now." It was intense, but that's what drew me in.
UNMISS armored vehicles on patrol
Q: How has this experience changed the way you see the world?
My worldview has completely opened up. I got to see a part of the world and meet people I never could have possibly encountered otherwise. These were experiences that were only possible through military service. For example, the civil affairs officer I worked with was a guy from Sierra Leone who had done his master's degree in Kenya. We'd talk for hours about his real estate business back home. The patrols further out of town with the Indian infantry were fascinating too — locals would follow us around, and I ended up making friends with the children in the villages. I actually got in trouble once for giving them food, because the policy was to prevent reliance on us. I was just handing out bags of chocolates.
Dylan with a local friend at the UN base in Pibor
Q: What are your plans now that you're transitioning back to civilian life?
It's going to take a minute to switch gears from military to civilian life. My plan is to attend summer school, travel a lot, and just gradually get used to being a regular member of society again. It's a bigger adjustment than people might think.
From the Field

The Drainage Pipe Incident

One day, a local man stole a drainage pipe from a project site because it was made of metal he could sell. He ended up selling it, and the pipe was eventually found broken down at someone else's house. We had to wait a month and a half for a replacement, and in the meantime, the roads were completely unusable. To make matters worse, the supply truck carrying the new pipe got raided on its way to us — along with a shipment of food. They robbed the car and took everything. That kind of thing happens all the time out there because there's only one major road connecting the two cities. The replacement truck eventually arrived completely empty.
